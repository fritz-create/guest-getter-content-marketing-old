# Brand Voice

**Brand:** Malt & Barley Public House
**Last updated:** 2026-04-22
**Source:** Brand Interview Section C + Research Output Section 4

---

## Voice Attributes

### Attribute 1: Genuine
- **IS:** Writing that sounds like a person who actually works here and knows what's in the pot. Specific, grounded, unpretentious.
- **IS NOT:** Marketing copy that could have been written about any pub in Ontario. Not performed warmth. Not "we pride ourselves."
- **In practice:** "The donair is ground beef and lamb on a vertical spit - kept as traditional as possible, which means sweet sauce, raw onions, and no shortcuts."

### Attribute 2: Local-first
- **IS:** Writing that assumes the reader already lives here. Williamsburg, Fischer-Hallman, KW - these are reference points, not explanations.
- **IS NOT:** Tourism copy. Not writing that introduces the city to an outsider. Not "located in the heart of Kitchener."
- **In practice:** "If you're in Williamsburg on a Tuesday night, you already know what trivia night looks like from the outside."

### Attribute 3: Quietly confident
- **IS:** Writing that trusts the food to speak. States what something is and why it matters, without overselling it.
- **IS NOT:** Hype. Not exclamation marks. Not "incredible," "amazing," or "you have to try this."
- **In practice:** "Sticky toffee pudding. It's on the dessert menu because it belongs there."

### Attribute 4: Unpretentious
- **IS:** Plain language. Short sentences. No food-media vocabulary. Pub writing, not restaurant week copy.
- **IS NOT:** Elevated, artisanal, farm-to-table, culinary journey, or any language that makes a pub sound like a tasting menu.
- **In practice:** "The fish and chips are East Coast. The batter is made here. That's it."

### Attribute 5: Community-rooted
- **IS:** Writing that acknowledges the pub is part of something - a neighbourhood, a weekly ritual, a group of regulars who didn't need to be marketed to.
- **IS NOT:** Trying to reach everyone. Not generic "come visit" language. Not tourism.
- **In practice:** "The kind of place the neighbourhood would miss if it were gone. We intend to keep it that way."

## Tone by Context

| Context | Tone Shift | Notes |
|---------|------------|-------|
| Blog posts | Conversational, informative, locally specific | Write like an insider. The reader already lives here. |
| GBP posts | Direct, warm, short | What's on, why it's worth coming for. No filler. |
| Event promotion | Enthusiastic but not hype-y | The event is real - describe it plainly. |
| Food descriptions | Specific and honest | Name ingredients, process, origin. Not adjectives. |
| Specials / deals | Plain and confident | "Wings for $13 every Wednesday." Not "INCREDIBLE DEAL." |
| Review responses | Grateful and genuine | Never defensive. Never corporate. First person "we." |

## Forbidden Language

**Words or phrases never used:**
- Em dashes - use a hyphen instead
- "Culinary journey" / "food journey" / "dining experience"
- "Elevate" / "elevated" / "artisanal"
- "Vibrant" / "bustling" / "thriving"
- "We pride ourselves on..."
- "Hidden gem"
- "Farm to table" (unless verifiably true for a specific item)
- "Second to none" / "something for everyone"
- "Come on down"

**Constructions to avoid:**
- Exclamation marks used more than once per piece
- ALL CAPS for emphasis
- Generic pub-speak that could apply to any bar in Ontario

**Topics that are never joked about:**
- Dietary restrictions or allergies (treat seriously, refer to staff)

---

## Self-Reference Rules

- The restaurant is referred to as: "Malt & Barley" on first reference, "we" / "the pub" / "the kitchen" thereafter
- Staff are referred to as: "the team" or by role (e.g., "the kitchen," "our bartenders") - not "our staff" or "our crew"
- Customers/guests are referred to as: "you" in direct address; "regulars" for established patrons; "locals" in neighbourhood context
- Menu items are formatted as: Plain (no quotes, no bold, no caps) - let the name stand on its own
